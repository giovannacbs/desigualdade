Overview of Women in the Brazilian Labor Market

The wage gap between men and women is a persistent reality throughout Brazil, but with different magnitudes. In this article, we will see the variations of this disparity by state, color and race and education level.

First of all, let's see how the labor market behaves in Brazil, what the participation of women in the labor market is, and what percentage of them are employed.

The first difference we will observe is in the salary inequality between the states: in places where the average salary is lower – click on the states on the map to see – the wage gap is not as significant as in states where the average salary is higher.

For example, in the Federal District, where the average salary exceeds R$4,900, men earn 37% more than women. On the other hand, in Amapá, where the average salary is around R$2,600, men earn 5% less than women.

When comparing the disparity between races and colors, we again see the phenomenon where the higher the average income, the greater the gap between what men and women earn, and vice versa.

The average salary for indigenous people is the lowest among all races – about R$2,020 – and the difference between the average salaries of an indigenous man and an indigenous woman in Brazil is smaller: 0.1%. However, when we make the same comparison for Asians, this difference reaches 41%.

When conducting the same type of analysis by education levels, we encounter the same result: higher education levels show greater inequality since lower levels receive average salaries closer to the minimum wage.
